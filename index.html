The globalisation of world cinema

A network and text analysis of roughly 22,000 films across a century of cinema, examining decade by decade whether film collaboration and film content became cross-national at the same rate.

Nicholas Borch (s234841) & Robin Braagaard (s234856) · Data · TheyShootPictures, TMDB & Wikipedia

Key findings

Summary of results

01

The gap exists, and it is inverted

Thematic ties cross borders in every decade, but co-starring ties almost never do. Cross-national edge share peaks at 16% on the collaboration side and stays above 35% on the thematic side throughout. Both move toward integration, but from opposite starting points: storytelling was already global, collaboration catches up slowly, carried by a minority of boundary-spanning actors who hold a disproportionate share of the network's structural connectivity.

02

Collaboration predicts content mostly through nationality

AMI(collaboration, theme) ≈ 0.15, of which the country-conditioned null accounts for 0.115. For our dataset, country explains 77% of the apparent link, and collaboration adds 23%. Co-starring communities are national; thematic communities are not; what little collaboration tells you about theme is mostly what nationality already told you.

03

Distinctiveness is real but fading

National cinemas carry distinctive vocabularies above the size-matched null in nearly every country–decade. The mean declines from ~0.10 in the 1930s to near zero by the 2020s, indicating convergence toward a shared global vocabulary. The caveat being that with ρ = −0.85 against plot length, much of the apparent fade may be uniform Wikipedia coverage rather than thematic convergence.

What we built it from

The dataset

The dataset should attribute every film to a national cinema reliably, list each film's full cast, and carry text rich enough to extract themes from. Finding a single public source containing this is practically impossible, therefore a step-wise dataset collection from three complementary sources is performed. TheyShootPictures gives century-wide coverage with lessened bias in terms of recency and western dominance; TMDB gives the structured cast that the co-starring network is built on; and Wikipedia gives the plots and themes texts behind the embeddings and the TF-IDF analysis. Actor birth-places were initially thought to be the backbone indication of actor nationality, but due to mainstream celebrity information sites being biased towards popular western actors, the actor nationality nodes were instead determined by the majority production country behind their movies.

The country distribution is starkly long-tailed and Anglophone-skewed: the USA alone produces ~8,400 films, more than the next four countries combined, with France, the UK, Japan, and Italy forming the rest of the top five. The decade distribution rises monotonically to a peak in the 2000s of around 3,500 films. Whether this is viewed as a property of the world being measured, or a limitation of a Western-biased source, can of course be debated — both are most likely true in some sense.

How the analysis works

The pipeline, step by step

The analysis proceeds in sequence, following the structure of the notebook: assembling the data, describing it, constructing the networks, and then addressing four research questions. Throughout, each reported trend is compared against a randomised null model, so that an observed effect can be distinguished from an artefact of data collection.

1

Data collection

We seed from a curated ranked film list, enrich each film with TMDB metadata and its full billed cast, resolve its plot through Wikidata and Wikipedia, and represent that text as 384-dimensional embedding vectors. Production country, known for almost every film, is the primary attribute; less complete signals such as actor birthplace are used only as flagged cross-checks.

2

Basic statistics

Before any modelling we examine the shape of the data: films per decade, producing countries, and cast-size and plot-length distributions. This is also where a collection artefact was identified and corrected, by requiring a co-starring tie to rest on at least two shared films rather than a single appearance.

distributionscoverage
3

Build the collaboration network

For each decade we link two actors who appeared together in at least two films, weighting the edge by the number of shared films, and tag each actor with the country in which they primarily worked. The result is a per-decade representation of on-screen collaboration.

graph constructionweighted edges
4

Measure the gap — Part 1

We measure how within-national each side is using country assortativity and the share of links that cross a border, on the collaboration graph and on a film-similarity graph built from plot vectors. Each value is benchmarked against a permutation null, so a rising cross-border share is not simply the result of more countries entering the data over time.

assortativitypermutation null
5

Brokers & corridors — Part 1

This step examines how cross-national collaboration occurs. Betweenness centrality identifies the internationally active actors that connect otherwise separate parts of the network, and the analysis traces the dominant country-to-country corridors that account for most cross-border casting.

6

National fingerprints — Part 2

Using TF-IDF over each country's pooled plot text, we surface the words a national cinema over-uses relative to world cinema. A size-matched null reports distinctiveness above noise, and a confound check guards against shorter plot summaries appearing more distinctive.

7

Community structure — Part 3

Louvain detection partitions each network into communities, which we compare to production country using adjusted mutual information. Collaboration communities are strongly national; thematic communities are largely not.

Bridging the lenses — Part 4

Finally we ask whether collaboration predicts content beyond the nationality the two already share. A country-conditioned null isolates that question, and a small but statistically robust association remains.

Do the trends hold up statistically

Conclusions

Each decade's value is benchmarked against a null, but that only tells us whether a single decade departs from chance and not whether the direction across decades is real. A Spearman rank test against decade fixes this: a strong ρ backs a "rose" or "fell" claim, a weak one says soften it. The partial 2020s is excluded so an incomplete decade doesn't distort the slope.

ρ +0.75

Collaboration cross-national share

The fraction of co-starring ties that link actors from different national cinemas. p = 0.008. Rises across the century; safe to claim.

ρ +0.81

Theme cross-national share

The fraction of film-similarity ties that link films from different national cinemas. p = 0.003. Rises across the century; safe to claim.

ρ +0.70

Boundary-spanner share

The fraction of actors with at least one foreign-cinema co-star. p = 0.017. Rises across the century; safe to claim.

ρ +0.33

Cross-national betweenness share

The fraction of total betweenness centrality held by boundary-spanning actors. p = 0.33. Jumps early (1920s → 1950s) then sits in the 0.3–0.6 range. The shape is real but not monotonic, so the test can't certify a trend.

ρ −0.81

Mean national distinctiveness

How far each country's TF-IDF fingerprint sits from the global mean, averaged across countries per decade. p = 0.001. Falls across the century; safe to claim.

Four of the five load-bearing trends survive. The fifth is consistent with the rest but cannot be defended as monotonic on its own. The project asks about world cinema, but the dataset is the slice of world cinema visible to three Western-curated, English-language sources. Country–decades with shorter plot summaries are reliably the more distinctive ones, and the longest-plot groups cluster near zero excess; the magnitudes of the distinctiveness numbers, and the steepness of the fading trend, are most likely driven a lot by Wikipedia coverage becoming more uniform over time rather than national cinemas converging thematically.
